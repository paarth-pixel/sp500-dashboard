How it works — indicators explained
Exponential Moving Average (EMA) Core concept

An EMA is the average price of a stock over a set number of days, but it gives more weight to recent prices. This makes it react faster to new price moves than a simple average. Formula: each day's EMA = (today's price × smoothing factor) + (yesterday's EMA × (1 − smoothing factor)), where the smoothing factor = 2 ÷ (period + 1). The result is a smooth line on a chart that follows the stock's trend direction.

20 EMA Short-term

Looks back over the last 20 trading days (roughly 1 month). Traders watch this as the short-term trend line. When price bounces off the 20 EMA, it often signals the trend is still healthy. When it breaks through, it can signal a short-term shift in momentum. This dashboard flags when price is within 1% of this level.

50 EMA Medium-term

Looks back over the last 50 trading days (roughly 2.5 months). This is one of the most widely-watched levels by professional traders. A stock holding above its 50 EMA is generally considered to be in a healthy medium-term uptrend. A break below it is often seen as a warning sign. Many institutional investors use this level as a key decision point.

200 EMA Long-term

Looks back over the last 200 trading days (roughly 10 months). This is the most important trend line in technical analysis. Price above the 200 EMA = long-term uptrend (bullish). Price below it = long-term downtrend (bearish). Major fund managers and analysts track this religiously. A stock falling below its 200 EMA often triggers large institutional sell orders.

RSI — Relative Strength Index Momentum

RSI measures how fast and how much a stock has moved over the last 14 days, on a scale of 0 to 100. Above 70 = overbought (the stock may have risen too fast and a pullback is possible). Below 30 = oversold (the stock may have fallen too hard and a bounce is possible). RSI doesn't tell you what will happen — it tells you the stock is at an extreme, and extremes tend to revert.

Golden Cross & Death Cross Crossover signals

These are events where two EMAs cross each other. A golden cross is when the 20 EMA crosses above the 50 EMA — a bullish signal that short-term momentum is turning positive. A death cross is the opposite: the 20 EMA crosses below the 50 EMA, a bearish signal. These are considered strong signals because they require a sustained shift in price momentum to occur.

Bullish Trend direction

A stock is labelled bullish when its current price is trading above its 200 EMA. This means the long-term trend is upward — the stock has on average been rising over the past 10 months. Bullish does not mean the stock will definitely go up. It means the underlying trend currently favours buyers over sellers. Most S&P 500 stocks are bullish in a healthy market.

Bearish Trend direction

A stock is labelled bearish when its current price is trading below its 200 EMA. This means the long-term trend is downward. It does not mean the stock is guaranteed to fall further — it may be in the early stages of recovery — but it signals that sellers have had more control than buyers over the past 10 months. Worth watching more carefully in a portfolio context.

Flagging logic — how alerts are triggered

A stock is flagged (shown in red) when any of these conditions are met: its current price is within 1% above or below its 20 EMA, 50 EMA, or 200 EMA; its RSI is above 70 (overbought) or below 30 (oversold); or a golden or death cross occurred on the most recent trading day. The 1% proximity threshold is standard in technical trading — it catches stocks approaching a key level before they actually hit it, giving a heads-up rather than a lagging signal.
